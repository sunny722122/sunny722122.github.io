Chapter Three

Harsh memory

Next morning felt thinner than usual, as if the air in the house had been used up overnight. Susan moved through the kitchen quietly, preparing breakfast, measuring each action so it stayed within routine. The children spoke about school, about small things that required simple answers. She responded where needed, but her attention drifted, returning again and again to the same point. The night. After the girls left for school, the house settled into a different kind of quiet. Ryane was already there, sitting at the table with a cup of coffee, his phone in his hand. His mother stood near the counter, rinsing something under running water. Susan didn’t plan to bring it up directly. Not at first. She started with something smaller—something ordinary enough to pass without consequence. “Did you hear anything last night?” she asked, keeping her voice even. “The door, maybe.” Ryane looked up briefly. “No.” His answer came too quickly, but without emphasis. His mother turned slightly, drying her hands. “I didn’t hear anything either,” she said. “I slept like a pig.” She laughed lightly after saying it, as if to soften the phrase. Ryane nodded, almost immediately. “Yeah. Same. Out cold.” The agreement between them was smooth, effortless. Susan stood still for a moment, her hand resting on the edge of the counter. The words themselves were ordinary. People said things like that all the time. But something in the timing—the way they answered, the ease with which they matched each other—pressed against her more than the content. Slept like a pig. The phrase repeated in her mind, not fading but sharpening. It should have reassured her. That was the purpose of it. To close the question, to leave no space for doubt. But instead, it did the opposite. It fixed the moment more clearly in her memory, gave it edges it hadn’t had before. If no one had heard anything, if nothing had happened, then why did the answer feel prepared? She nodded, as if satisfied. “Okay,” she said. The conversation moved on. Something about groceries. About a bill that needed to be paid. The ordinary flow of the morning resumed, carrying them all forward without pause. But inside, the moment stayed. Not large. Not dramatic. Just precise. As if something had been said not to explain the past, but to shape how it would be remembered.

Two months later, they bought a second house. The decision had a practical explanation. Susan wanted her parents to visit, just for a short time. It had been years. She could not bring the children back to see them—not after everything that had happened before, things she no longer tried to revisit in detail. So she arranged the alternative: a place where her parents could come without entering the tension that had settled permanently into their current home. That was what she said aloud. What she did not say, even to herself in complete sentences, was that she needed another place. Something separate. Something that could exist independently of Ryane, even if only in potential. A place that might, at some point, become hers.

At the beginning of the marriage, she had not thought this way. Back then, she had trusted him completely. It had seemed natural to let him manage everything—finances, accounts, decisions that required planning beyond the immediate. She handed things over gradually, then entirely, without marking the moment it became total. It felt efficient. It felt like partnership. Later, when she worked abroad for a period of time, something shifted. Distance made certain patterns visible. She stopped sending all her earnings back. At first it was small—keeping a portion, then more. Not out of defiance, but out of a growing sense that she should have something of her own. The arguments began almost immediately. They were not about money alone, though money was always the surface. Beneath it was something less negotiable: control, expectation, a structure that had already been decided without being named. The conversations escalated quickly, repeating themselves with slight variations, never resolving. At times they crossed into something physical—brief, contained, but enough to leave a mark that lasted longer than the moment itself. Those years accumulated. They did not arrive all at once, but layered gradually, until the decision to divorce no longer felt sudden. It felt overdue. Ryane refused. He did not argue the reasons. He dismissed the outcome. Divorce, to him, was not a conclusion to reach but a condition to prevent. Keeping the marriage intact—formally, structurally—seemed to matter more than what remained inside it. Looking back, Susan sometimes thought that was why his mother had come to stay. Not only to help, not only for convenience, but to stabilize something that was beginning to shift beyond his control. Another presence in the house. Another set of eyes. Another reinforcement of what the household was supposed to be. Life, outwardly, had begun to settle again not long before the second house purchase. Susan had found a comparatively stable job. The routines of work and school had re-established themselves. From the outside, things might have looked functional.

It had been six years. Six years since she had seen her parents in person. She thought about them often, but always within limits, as if allowing the thought to extend too far would make the distance harder to manage. Bringing them here felt like the only viable option left. A short visit. A contained period of time. She told herself it was for that reason. But underneath, another memory remained—quieter, harder to place. A time when Ryane had used their daughter’s presence as leverage. Not directly, not in a way that could be easily repeated or proven, but clearly enough that Susan understood the implication. The message had not needed to be stated fully to be effective. Since then, certain decisions no longer felt like choices. The second house offered something small but measurable: distance that could be accounted for. Space that could be entered or left with intention. A boundary that did not depend entirely on agreement. When the purchase was completed, there was no sense of relief—only a subtle shift. As if a line had been drawn—not visible, not acknowledged—but present all the same.
