Chapter Three

Harsh memory

Next morning felt thinner than usual, as if the air in the house had been used up overnight. Susan moved through the kitchen quietly, preparing breakfast, measuring each action so it stayed within routine. The children spoke about school, about small things that required simple answers. She responded where needed, but her attention drifted, returning again and again to the same point. The night. After the girls left for school, the house settled into a different kind of quiet. Ryane was already there, sitting at the table with a cup of coffee, his phone in his hand. His mother stood near the counter, rinsing something under running water. Susan didn’t plan to bring it up directly. Not at first. She started with something smaller—something ordinary enough to pass without consequence. “Did you hear anything last night?” she asked, keeping her voice even. “The door, maybe.” Ryane looked up briefly. “No.” His answer came too quickly, but without emphasis. His mother turned slightly, drying her hands. “I didn’t hear anything either,” she said. “I slept like a pig.” She laughed lightly after saying it, as if to soften the phrase. Ryane nodded, almost immediately. “Yeah. Same. Out cold.” The agreement between them was smooth, effortless. Susan stood still for a moment, her hand resting on the edge of the counter. The words themselves were ordinary. People said things like that all the time. But something in the timing—the way they answered, the ease with which they matched each other—pressed against her more than the content. Slept like a pig. The phrase repeated in her mind, not fading but sharpening. It should have reassured her. That was the purpose of it. To close the question, to leave no space for doubt. But instead, it did the opposite. It fixed the moment more clearly in her memory, gave it edges it hadn’t had before. If no one had heard anything, if nothing had happened, then why did the answer feel prepared? She nodded, as if satisfied. “Okay,” she said. The conversation moved on. Something about groceries. About a bill that needed to be paid. The ordinary flow of the morning resumed, carrying them all forward without pause. But inside, the moment stayed. Not large. Not dramatic. Just precise. As if something had been said not to explain the past, but to shape how it would be remembered.

Two months later, they bought a second house. The decision had a practical explanation. Susan wanted her parents to visit, just for a short time. It had been years. She could not bring the children back to see them—not after everything that had happened before, things she no longer tried to revisit in detail. So she arranged the alternative: a place where her parents could come without entering the tension that had settled permanently into their current home. That was what she said aloud. What she did not say, even to herself in complete sentences, was that she needed another place. Something separate. Something that could exist independently of Ryane, even if only in potential. A place that might, at some point, become hers.

At the beginning of the marriage, she had not thought this way. Back then, she had trusted him completely. It had seemed natural to let him manage everything—finances, accounts, decisions that required planning beyond the immediate. She handed things over gradually, then entirely, without marking the moment it became total. It felt efficient. It felt like partnership. Later, when she worked abroad for a period of time, something shifted. Distance made certain patterns visible. She stopped sending all her earnings back. At first it was small—keeping a portion, then more. Not out of defiance, but out of a growing sense that she should have something of her own. The arguments began almost immediately. They were not about money alone, though money was always the surface. Beneath it was something less negotiable: control, expectation, a structure that had already been decided without being named. The conversations escalated quickly, repeating themselves with slight variations, never resolving. At times they crossed into something physical—brief, contained, but enough to leave a mark that lasted longer than the moment itself. Those years accumulated. They did not arrive all at once, but layered gradually, until the decision to divorce no longer felt sudden. It felt overdue. Ryane refused. He did not argue the reasons. He dismissed the outcome. Divorce, to him, was not a conclusion to reach but a condition to prevent. Keeping the marriage intact—formally, structurally—seemed to matter more than what remained inside it. Looking back, Susan sometimes thought that was why his mother had come to stay. Not only to help, not only for convenience, but to stabilize something that was beginning to shift beyond his control. Another presence in the house. Another set of eyes. Another reinforcement of what the household was supposed to be. Life, outwardly, had begun to settle again not long before the second house purchase. Susan had found a comparatively stable job. The routines of work and school had re-established themselves. From the outside, things might have looked functional.

It had been six years. Six years since she had seen her parents in person. She thought about them often, but always within limits, as if allowing the thought to extend too far would make the distance harder to manage. Bringing them here felt like the only viable option left. A short visit. A contained period of time. She told herself it was for that reason. But underneath, another memory remained—quieter, harder to place. A time when Ryane had used their daughter’s presence as leverage. Not directly, not in a way that could be easily repeated or proven, but clearly enough that Susan understood the implication. The message had not needed to be stated fully to be effective. Since then, certain decisions no longer felt like choices. The second house offered something small but measurable: distance that could be accounted for. Space that could be entered or left with intention. A boundary that did not depend entirely on agreement. When the purchase was completed, there was no sense of relief—only a subtle shift. As if a line had been drawn—not visible, not acknowledged—but present all the same.

Right before her parents arrived, Susan developed a bladder infection. It began the way it always did—subtly, then all at once. A faint discomfort she recognized immediately, followed by the familiar urgency that made it impossible to ignore. She didn’t hesitate this time. Instead of waiting, she booked a virtual appointment. The doctor appeared on the screen, efficient and neutral. The questions followed a pattern Susan already knew. “Have you been drinking enough water?” “Yes,” she answered. That part was simple. She had always been careful about it. It was one of the few things she controlled without interruption. “Any recent sexual activity that might have triggered this?” The question landed differently. Susan paused, just briefly. She had experienced this before—many times, years earlier. Back then, the cause had been clear. The pattern had repeated often enough that she no longer needed it explained. She knew her body. She knew what led to what. But that had been before. Now, there should be no reason. She and Ryane had been separated for a long time, even if they still lived under the same roof. There had been no contact. Not recently. Not for months. The possibility should have been zero. On the screen, the doctor waited, patient but expectant. Susan looked at her own reflection in the corner of the call window. Her face appeared composed, unchanged. There was nothing visible to explain the hesitation. “No,” she said finally. The doctor nodded and moved on, listing treatment options, explaining dosage, asking a few more routine questions. The appointment ended quickly. Efficient. Resolved, at least on paper. Susan closed the laptop and sat still for a moment. The explanation remained incomplete. She told herself there were other causes. There were always other causes. Bacteria didn’t need a clear narrative. Bodies didn’t always follow logic. She had been under stress. Her routine had changed. It could be anything. Still, the earlier question lingered. Not because of what she had said, but because of what she hadn’t. She had chosen not to explain the uncertainty, not to describe the gap between what she knew and what she felt. The thought of saying it aloud—to a stranger, through a screen—felt impossible. It would sound fragmented, ungrounded. It would invite a different kind of explanation, one she had already encountered. She didn’t want to be redirected again. So she kept it to herself. And in doing so, something settled more deeply—not clarity, not certainty, but a quiet recognition that parts of her experience no longer had a place to be spoken. Not outside. Only within.

About a week later, the doctor followed up. By then, the symptoms were gone. The medication had worked quickly, as expected. On the surface, the issue had been resolved. This time, Susan didn’t keep everything to herself. There was a pause in the conversation—longer than before—before she spoke. She chose her words carefully, trying to keep them grounded, factual, as if precision might make them more acceptable. “I’ve had this before,” she said. “Usually… it happens after certain situations. But that shouldn’t apply now.” The doctor waited. Susan continued, more quietly. “I’m not sure how to explain it, but I don’t think this just… happened randomly.” She didn’t say everything. Not directly. But the implication was there, fragile but present. On the screen, the doctor’s expression shifted—not dismissive, not alarmed, but more attentive than before. “It’s important,” the doctor said, “that if you suspect something may have happened without your awareness, you seek immediate care next time. Your body can provide evidence—but only within a certain time frame.” Susan listened without interrupting. “You can go to the emergency department,” the doctor continued. “They can do an assessment, collect samples if needed, and document findings properly. But timing matters. The sooner, the better.” The words settled slowly. Evidence. The idea felt both solid and distant at the same time. She hadn’t known this before. Not the first time. Not this time either. Both moments had already passed. She nodded, though the movement felt delayed, as if it belonged to someone else. “Okay,” she said. After the call ended, she sat still, her hands resting in her lap. It wasn’t just that she had missed the window. It was that she hadn’t known there was a window. The absence of that knowledge now felt like another kind of gap—one that couldn’t be corrected retroactively. Even if something had happened, she had no way to confirm it. And even if she tried to explain it now, what would she be presenting? A pattern without proof. A certainty without evidence. She thought about the house. About the shared walls, the unlocked door—at least until recently. The fact that separation, in practice, had never meant distance. Only arrangement. Clarifying anything within that space would not be simple. It might not be possible. Still, one thing had changed. If it happened again, she would know where to go. The thought did not comfort her. It sat somewhere between preparation and resignation, like learning the location of an exit in a building she did not feel safe in. Later that day, almost without deciding to, she thought about the counselor. She had told her everything. Not all at once, but enough. Enough that the outline should have been visible. And yet, the direction had always turned inward—toward stress, toward perception, toward the possibility that what she experienced originated from within her own mind. Susan understood why. It was the safer explanation. The more manageable one. But now, sitting alone, the contrast sharpened. One person had suggested doubt. Another had suggested evidence. The space between those two responses felt wider than it should have been. And within that space, Susan found herself increasingly careful. Not only about what she said—but about who she said it to. Because the pattern had become clear in a way she hadn’t expected: The more she tried to explain, the less certain she appeared to others. And the less certain she appeared, the further away help seemed to move. So she began, quietly, to hold more of it inside. Not because it was resolved. But because it no longer felt safe to place it elsewhere.
